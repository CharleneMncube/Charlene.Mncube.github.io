Civil Engineeringand and Beyond

I am now well within 3 years of my career as an engineer. The reality is that not much of the picture I had envisioned of innovation is happening in the industry yet. However, I work in the offshoring unit where I largely work on Australian and New Zealand transportation projects. Working on world-class projects with some of the best engineering minds in the world has been surreal, but there is still a disconnect of working on first-world projects while living in a developing country. Although we used some sophisticated engineering software to design-built environment infrastructure, there were some grey areas and room for more efficient solutions both on-site and in the office, and I observed that there was a market for it, especially in Africa. The tools we used in the offshoring unit were way more sophisticated than the Africa business. Of which I do realise a lot of these efficiency solutions are a nice to have, especially for a country still facing BASIC infrastructure and asset management inadequacies. Last year, I had the opportunity to present on an emerging technology in the roads and transportation industry of my choice for our company's roads learning week. I presented on the value propositions of adopting the digital twin model in transportation systems. I further realized and concluded that it wasn't the engineering of the road that I was most interested in, but rather the development of tech solutions and systems to make the road and its systems more efficient that intrigued me the most. My identity as an engineer is an emotional one. I will forever be an engineer at heart, regardless of which industry I may transition to. And Elon Musk does admit we do make the best CEOs, so that's a pursuit worth charting. The skills and knowledge acquisitions I have garnered as an engineer are priceless, not only in the tools we use in the office, but also in the way we think, even outside of the office, we are intrinsic solutionists. I do wish we were more valued in the industry than we currently are in South Africa. Hence, the brain drains professionals. There's a synonymous saying that engineers are more valued outside of engineering than they are in engineering. And I hope that changes soon. We can't fully place the blame on engineering firms, but there is a blatant lack of funds for the industry to fully thrive which trickles down to the young aspiring engineer with big dreams. The reality is that our country needs engineers, the civil engineering industry needs funding, and a vote of confidence from the government too.
