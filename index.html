Intro

Charlene is a seasoned Engineering Consultant, with extensive experience working on transportation infrastructure projects. She has a demonstrated background in problem-solving, data management, and programming.

Throughout her career, she has contributed to a diverse range of world-class infrastructure initiatives in collaboration with colleagues from Africa, Australasia, South-East Asia, and the Middle East. Her expertise spans project management, design, modeling, drafting, project finance, and reporting on major infrastructure projects.

Driven by a passion for developing innovative solutions, Charlene excels at addressing complex business challenges, particularly through leveraging digital transformation to promote sustainable infrastructure development. This passion fueled her final-year research project, which examined the impact of the Fourth Industrial Revolution on the Civil Engineering industry in South Africa. The project deepened her commitment to exploring innovation adoption and its implications for engineering and technology, especially within African communities.

In addition to her technical expertise, Charlene is known for her strong interpersonal skills, engaging comfortably with people from all walks of life—whether in boardrooms with executives or at casual social events with colleagues.

Those who know her well describe her as conscientious, witty, sociable, self-aware, ambitious, empathetic, and candid.

My Journey

A TUMULTUOUS START

| 2017 – 2019

After matric, I went wayward, which put me in a situation where I ended up taking a gap year. During that time, I attempted to enroll in the National Defence Force to realize my teenage dream of 'working for the CIA,' which I quickly reconsidered. I ended up working at a restaurant, where I was able to save up some money to take myself to university to study engineering. Needless to say, that experience helped me build real-world resilience and independence. Serendipity knocked at the door, and I was fortunate to receive sponsorships, which helped me realize my dream of becoming an engineer.

| 2020
During my final year of studies, I was uncertain about which career path to pursue as a young engineer, given the variety of options available. Sponsored by a private bank, I gained exposure to the banking industry and discovered roles within the financial services sector where my engineering skills could be utilized. Despite the surge in the data science and analytics field, I chose to continue with the traditional engineering path. I used my extra time to learn after hours. Despite the challenges posed by remote work and demanding coursework, I successfully completed the program, gaining expertise in various data management, gathering, and visualization tools, as well as machine learning systems and the entire data science pipeline. Now, I was faced with the decision of what to do next?.

Civil Engineering and and Beyond

| 2013 - Present
I am now well within 5 years of my career as an engineer - 10 000 hours - yes?, and I've noticed that not much of the innovation I envisioned has been effectively implemented locally in the industry yet. However, the industry is steadily starting to recognize the importance of optimizing their business with technology. Currently, I work in the offshoring unit on transportation projects in Australia and New Zealand, which has provided an incredible opportunity to collaborate with some of the industry's best engineering minds. Nevertheless, the disconnect between working on first-world projects while living in a developing country emphasizes the discrepancy and highlights how much further we need to go as a continent to achieve world-class sustainable and innovative infrastructure. Though we use sophisticated engineering software, there is still room for more efficient solutions both on-site and in the office. Recently, I presented the digital twin model for our company's roads learning week and realized my interest lies in unearthing innovative solutions to solve complex business problems. As an engineer, I have acquired invaluable skills and knowledge, which makes me an intrinsic problem solver. Unfortunately, engineers are undervalued in South Africa, leading to the current brain drain. The industry needs adequate funding and support from an innovation fund to fully thrive. As a young professional, I will continue to adapt and evolve with the disruptions and apply my diverse skills to make a global impact.
